Description of Source

B’Tselem, The Israeli Information Centre for Human Rights in the Occupied Territories believes in creating a future in which human rights, liberty and equality are guaranteed to all people, living between the Jordan River and the Mediterranean Sea. The name, B’Tselem, means in the image of in Hebrew. This was decided by the late Member of Knesset Yossi Sarid to express the universal and Jewish moral edict to respect and uphold the human rights of all people. They document and educate the public about human rights violations in the Israeli-occupied territories, including the West Bank, East Jerusalem, and Gaza Strip.

Field research, documentation, legal advocacy, and public outreach were methods to reveal and confront issues such as illegal executions, deprivation of property or land, and restrictions on freedom of movement. The organization has previously faced controversy and criticism where they are were accused of being biased against Israel or for undermining Israeli security. However, some argues that they play a crucial role in holding Israeli authorities accountable for their actions as well as encouraging respect for human rights and international law.

The website, Israel-Palestine Timeline, provides a timeline documenting every Palestinian and Israeli killed in the middle of the conflict since September 2000. This website is a project of If Americans Knew, which is a nonprofit organisation committed to gather information about the U.S. connection to Israel-Palestine for Americans. Their goal is to create a page with information and images for each person who has been killed. The data on this website was taken from B’Tselem, which is also one of the sources used in this project, the International Media Information Centre (IMEMC), as well as additional reports from news agencies such as, Ma’an News and Ha’aretz. On the website, they emphasised that these data may not be reported fully due to limited access by reporting agencies.

Kaggle is often described as an AirBnB for data scientists. The website provides a platform for enthusiasts to refine their skills and tackle data science challenges in real-world setting. Kaggle started in Melbourne and later moved to Silicon Valley. They received several notable investors before being acquired by Google in 2017. The platform offers competitions ranging from predicting cancer occurrence to analysing how movies affect audience reaction, code sharing and dataset hosting. The community is fueled with high quality content and active engagement within users, making Kaggle the go-to platform for data scientists and machine learning engineers from all over the world.

Humanitarian Data Exchange (HDX) is an online platform for data sharing across crises and organisations. HDX is managed by the United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) and its goal is to make humanitarian data easy to find and use for analysis. The website offers access to various datasets, tools and resources contributed by humanitarian organisations and governments. These data cover various factors such as demographics, health, nutrition, water and sanitisation, and all are free and accessible to users worldwide, encouraging transparency, collaboration and transformation. At the same time, users would be able to access tools for data analysis, exploration and visualisation, allowing them to gain insights and make informed decisions. HDX enables coordination and communication among researchers to effectively exchange knowledge and address humanitarian challenges.

Collection of Data

B’Tselem collects data through various methods to document human rights violations in the Israeli-occupied territories. One of these approaches include field research. B’Tselem has a team of research and fieldworkers that carry out on-site investigations to gather information about related topics. They would visit affected areas to interview victims, witnesses and residents. Testimonies and evidence would also be collected, which includes photographs, videos and documents. Furthermore, they would collaborate with local organisations, activist, lawyers and community leaders to gather materials and share resources. They also would work with international human rights associations, journalists and researchers to exchange information and increase their impact.

The website collects their data from several sources. One of these sources is B’Tselem, an Israeli non-profit organisation that documents human rights abuses in the occupied Palestinian territories. Another source that was mainly used is International Media Information Centre. This is an independent news organisation that reports on happenings in both Israel and the Palestinian territories. In addition, the website also collects information and reports from the news sites, Ma’an News and Ha’aretz, about the deaths in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.

Kaggle access and collect data through various ways to create a diverse database for its users to access and analyse. The main method of data collection is by user contributions. Users are responsible for sourcing and uploading data from various external sources. By approaching this collaboratively, it allows a wide range of dataset from different origin, cultivating a rich and diverse collection of data available for Kaggle’s users. Users may implement data cleaning and preprocessing to enhance usefulness of the datasets before sharing them. Other than this, some of the dataset offered exclusively would be sourced externally. This may be from data providers and publicly available sources.

HDX collects data by using several sources that ranges from government organisations to local communities to provide a thorough view of humanitarian crises and effective assistances. Some of the data are contributions from humanitarian organisations, which contributes to the information on relevant aspects of humanitarian disasters and issues. In addition, HDX collaborates with academic institutions and research organisations to gather studies related to humanitarian crises. These datasets would contribute to a deeper understanding of the underlying causes of disaster. Another way HDX collects data is by crowd sourcing and citizen reporting. These involves gathering information directly from affected communities or individuals, contributing insights into the impacts during crises.

Reliability of Source

A website named, ‘Media Bias / Fact Check’, promotes awareness and misinformation of media bias. It gives a detailed report on B’Tselem’s bias and credibility. The website rated B’Tselem as left-wing bias. They may use wordings that aims to influence readers’ emotions, publish misleading information and excludes any information that might have a negative impact on liberal causes. According to Media Bias/Fact Check, B’Tselem provides mostly factual reports with a medium credibility.

Another site described B’Tselem as ‘less reliability and credibility but more politics.’ Jerusalem Center for Public Affairs (JCPA) is an independent research institute producing studies and initiatives on a wide range of strategic topics. The article about B’Tselem suggested that they may be unfair and trustworthy due to their dependence on fundings and information from politically biased groups. The JCPA has their doubts on the legitimacy of B’Tselem’s way of observation and draws attention to their influence on anti-Israel campaigns.

As there was absence of additional resources commenting on the reliability of this website, the reliability would be determined by the creator of this website. The creator, If Americans Knew, is considered as a left-centre biased source, according to the Media Bias/Fact Check website. This means that If Americans Knew has a slight to moderate liberal bias. It has been suggested that they would often provide information with wording that may influence readers’ emotions to favour liberal causes. With a factual reporting rating of high, they are said to be trustworthy in general, however, may require further examination. Media Bias/Fact Check rated If American Knew as highly credible with the utilisation of several different sources.

Regarding the reliability of Kaggle, there are websites and forums where users discuss their experiences and opinions on the datasets and competitions. However, there are no websites that solely focus on assessing Kaggle’s reliability. Most of the time, users on Kaggle rely on community feedback, ratings and reviews from others within Kaggle to determine the quality and reliability of the datasets.

There was minimal information on the reliability of HDX on the internet. Therefore, this section would be based on a review written on Spatial Reserves and focusing on the reliability of its organiser, OCHA.

The review by Joseph Kerski on their website, Spatial Reserves, focuses on giving a guide to public domain special data. HDX is praised for its distinctive and fascinating function that allows users to clean and visualise their data with tools that work with the Humanitarian Exchange Language (HXL). These functions include the ability to give assistance in tagging others’ data, spotting errors and creating data visualisation. While the review highlights the strengths of HDX providing relevant and accessible data, it does not specifically discuss about the reliability of the data. However, the association of reputable organisations such as OCHA may indicate a level of credibility and trustworthiness in the data.

On this note, the page from NGO Monitor’s website discusses the bias they perceive within OCHA in the occupied Palestinian territories. It suggests that there are concerns regarding the objectivity and neutrality of the reports on humanitarian crises in the region. At the same time, it believes that OCHA allegedly focus unreasonably on Israel while overlooking Palestinian violations. Other than criticism on OCHA’s lack of transparency in its funding and methods, the website also suggests that their actions may have a biased narrative regarding the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.
